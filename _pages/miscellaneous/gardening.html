When I moved to Oxford, I took over an allotment and have been maintaining it for the past four years. Some things were successes, some were failures, some I am yet to find out what will happen! Below some thoughts.

My experience growing amaryllises from scratch (in progress)

My Mum gave me amaryllis bulbs as a souvenir from our trip to Amsterdam. I have planted them, grown them, and even pollinated them to harvest their seeds! I'm expecting that I will have multiple amaryllises in... five years or so. I thought of keeping this page as a record book of the steps. Who knows, if someone is as forward-looking as me, maybe they will be tempted by this experiment! I do not claim to be an expert, but here is what I did.

First things first, how to wake up a bulb? Results from my (limited sample) experiment

There are lots of ways to approach waking up the bulb. Upon buying, the bulb pretty much looks like an onion, with a grey layer of dried cover outside, and dried roots at the bottom. I tried various things, and this is what I found effective:

- I peeled the old layer. This is not mandatory, I just think it looks aesthetically nice.

- I cut (!) the bottom of the base, at 0.5cm. Ruthless, I know, BUT I have found that the grey roots are old, dead roots, they won't wake up.

- I put this newly cut bulb in some water, such that only the base is immersed at 1cm.

- I then waited a couple of days, so that new roots grew. They did!!

When the bulb is awaken, planting it:

I then put the bulbs in a pot, not too large because apparently amaryllises like to be in a narrow space to thrive. At the bottom of the pot, I put clay balls to allow draining. I then mixed 50% normal soil, 50% sand, and covered the bottom.

On top of this small layer, I added the bulb, covered with this mixture, stopped around "the shoulder" of the bulb (see picture attached). The bulb does not need to be covered to the top.

Then I watered well. And I waited.

I watered once a week, every week. After a couple of days, I could already see the first flower stem

After a couple of days, two flowers were on the rise, and they delighted me for weeks after that!

When the flowers are here, time to pollinate!

Time to think about amaryllis reproduction! Turns out I had two types of flowers, one pink and white, and another red! So I decided to multiply the red ones to have lots of small bulbs for the future. What an endeavour.

First take a small brush, and tap it on the stamen to get the pollen onto the brush.

Once you have pollen onto the brush, tap again on the stigma.

And now you wait. And you continue watering every week.

It will take a couple of weeks for the flower to die off. Once this happens, the stem flower pot will grow.

Then you have to wait until everything is dried off, at some point the stem flower pot will open, and dozens of seeds will fall off! You have your little baby amaryllises ready to be planted!

Note that after that, you still have to water the (Mother) amaryllis, otherwise it will die. Indeed, the bulb lost a lot of resources by (i) producing a flower (ii) producing seeds, and if you touch the bulb, you will see that it is much softer now. So keep watering, once a week or so, until leaves start to grow. Thanks to this, the bulb will get its strength back.

After that, you can wait until it feels like the bulb has gained its strength again. At that point you stop watering. Personally, I stopped watering in August.

Stopping watering triggers the dormancy period, where the sap goes back into the bulb. The leaves will turn yellow. I did not cut them. I cut them when they were completely dried.

Planting the new seeds

With my new seeds at hand, I started off by planting them in very small pots (same story as before, they like being in narrow spaces).

I watered, and a week later, I saw baby leaves starting to grow!!!

Since then, I have been watering every week. Six months later, I could start to see some bulbs. As of now, a year later, they are 1cm diameter... far from the 10-12cm diameter the parents are, in order to be able to give flowers.
